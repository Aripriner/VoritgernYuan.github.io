Covering Spaces

Posted on 2022-07-01 In Summary

Here I want to summarize the basic results of covering spaces. My reference book is May’s A Concise Course in Algebraic Topology.

The definition of covering spaces

Definition. A map $p: E \rightarrow B$ is a covering if it is surjective and if each point $b \in B$ has an open neighborhood $V$ such that each component of $p^{-1}(V)$ is open in $E$ and is mapped homeomorphically onto $V$ by $p$. We say that a path connected open subset $V$ with this property is a fundamental neighborhood of $B$. We call $E$ the total space, $B$ the base space, and $F_b=p^{-1}(b)$ a fiber of the covering $p$.

The unique path lifting property

Theorem(Unique path lifting). Let $p:E\rightarrow B$ be a covering, let $b\in B$, and let $e,e’\in F_b$.

  1. A path $f:I\rightarrow B$ with $f(0)=b$ lifts uniquely to a path $g:I \rightarrow E$ such that $g(0)=e$ and $p\circ q=f$.
  2. Equivalent paths $f\simeq f’:I\rightarrow B$ that start at $b$ lift to eqyivalent paths $g\simeq g’:I\rightarrow E$ that start at $e$, hence $g(1)=g’(1)$.
  3. $p_*:\pi_1(E,e) \rightarrow \pi_1(B,b)$ is a monomorphism.
  4. $p_(\pi_1(E,e))$ is conjugate to $p_(\pi_1(E,e’))$.
  5. As $e’$ runs through $F_b$, the groups $p_(\pi_1(E,e’))$ all conjugates of $p_(\pi_1(E,e))$ in $\pi_1(B,b)$.

Covering of groupoids

Definition. Let $\mathscr{E}$ and $\mathscr{B}$ be small connected groupoids. A covering $p: \mathscr{E}\rightarrow \mathscr{B}$ is a functor that is surjective on objects and restricts to a bijection

for each object $e$ of $\mathscr{E}$. For an object $b$ of $\mathscr{B}$, let $F_b$ denote the set of objects of $\mathscr{E}$ such that $p(e)=b$. Then $p^{-1}(St(b))$ is the disjoint union over $e\in F_b$ of $St(e)$.

Lemma. Let $G$ act transitively on a set $S$, choose $s \in S$, and let $H=G_s$. Then $WH=N_G(H)/H$ is isomorphic to the group $Aut_G(S)$ of the $G$-set $S$.

Lemma. The category $\mathscr{O}(G)$ is isomorphic to the category $\mathscr{G}$ whose objects are the subgoups of $G$ and whose morphisms are the distinct subconjugacy relations $\gamma^{-1}H\gamma\subset K$ for $\gamma \in G$.

Theorem. Let $p:\mathscr{E}\rightarrow \mathscr{B}$ be a covering of groupoids, let $\mathscr{X}$ be a groupoid, anf let $f:\mathscr{X} \rightarrow \mathscr{B}$ be functor. Choose a base object $x_0 \in \mathscr{X}$, let $b_0=f(x_0)$, and choose $e_0 \in F_{b_0}$. Then there is a functor $g:\mathscr{X}\rightarrow\mathscr{E}$ such that $g(x_0)=e_0$ and $p\circ g =f$ if and only if

in $\pi_1(\mathscr{B},b_0)$. When this condition holds, there is a unique such functor $g$.

Theorem. Let $p:\mathscr{E}\rightarrow\mathscr{B}$ and $p’:\mathscr{E}’\rightarrow \mathscr{B}$ be coverings, choose a base object $b \in \mathscr{B}$, and let $G=\pi_1(\mathscr{B},b_0)$. If $g:\mathscr{E}\rightarrow\mathscr{E}’$ is a map of coverings, then $g$ restricts to a map $F_b\rightarrow F_b’$ of $G$-sets,and restriction of fibers specifies a bijection between $Cov(\mathscr{E},\mathscr{E}’)$ and the set of $G$- maps $F_b\rightarrow F_b’$.

A Problem about Field Extension

Posted on 2022-06-26 In Problem

Today I meet a problem from Serge Lang’s Algebra:

Let E be a finite extension of k and let $p^r=[E:k]_i$. We assume that the characteristic is $p>0$. Assume that there is no exponent $p^s$ with $s>r$ such that $E^{p^s}k$ is separable over k(i.e.,such that $\alpha^{p^s}$ is separable over k for each $\alpha$ in E). Show that E can be generated by one element over k.

The key step of the problem is the lemma below:

k is an infinite field. $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are algebraic over k. And $\alpha$ is separable over k. Then $k(\alpha,\beta)$ can be generated by one element.

We will give a brief outline of the proof.Denote $s=t\alpha+\beta$. Denote f(x) and g(x) as the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ over k . Then $g(s-tx)$ has a root $\alpha$. So the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $k(s)$ divides $g(s-tx)$. If $\alpha \notin k(s)$, for $\alpha$ is separable, the degree of the common factor of $f(x)$ and $g(s-tx)$ is not smaller than 2. We have $\alpha’$ and $\beta’$ such that $t\alpha+\beta=t\alpha’+\beta’$. So

Now we know that the number of $\alpha’,\beta’$ is finite..But the choice of t is infinite. So we must have a t suche that $\alpha \in k(\alpha+t\beta)$ for some t. Finally $\alpha+t\beta$ is what we need.

I think the skill used in the proof is standard but interesting if you first see it. I like it.
